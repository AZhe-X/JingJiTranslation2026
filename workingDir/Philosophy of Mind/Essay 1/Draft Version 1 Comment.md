## Domain-Specific Comments

### 1. Emergence and Levels of Description
The essay's core move — using renormalization to argue for "effective existence" of macro-level phenomena — maps well onto philosophy of mind debates about:
- **Multiple realizability**: Different individual configurations, same collective property
- **Supervenience**: Collective interest supervenes on but isn't reducible to individual interests
- **Non-reductive physicalism**: Macro-phenomena are real without being identical to micro-descriptions

**Translation handles this well.** The parenthetical "(in the way that mathematics does)" is preserved, which is important — it signals the author's view that emergent phenomena are real but in a particular sense.

### 2. Empathy and Other Minds
The passage on empathy is philosophically significant:

> "it is only because we are human individuals that we can truly have a chance to understand the joys and sorrows of other human individuals"

This touches on the **other minds problem** and **phenomenological intersubjectivity**. The author's claim is that shared ontological status (being an individual) grounds empathy — we project from our own felt experience. This aligns with simulation theory of mind-reading.

**Translation is accurate.** The phrase "joys and sorrows" (悲欢) captures the phenomenal dimension.

### 3. Cognitive Accessibility
The author's point that individuals "cannot take a stand upon something that transcends themselves" relates to the **limits of first-person access**. We can only cognize from within our experiential domain.

**Note:** Footnote 1's translation ("it is only by standing upon oneself that transcending oneself can be defined at all") captures the reflexive structure nicely.
