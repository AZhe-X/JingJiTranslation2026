## 2. A Thought on Knowledge

### Summary
The author examines the classical JTB (justified true belief) definition of knowledge and argues that, in practice, it reduces to belief alone. Truth is methodologically inaccessible (we only access phenomena); justification either regresses infinitely, circulates, or bottoms out in unjustified belief. Therefore, what we call "knowledge" is actually "what each individual believes to be correct."

### Key Arguments

1. **Belief is intrinsically subjective**: Belief is "taking something as true." Any connection to "objective truth" is *extrinsic* â€” it requires something beyond belief itself to evaluate.

2. **Truth is methodologically inaccessible**: We verify claims against *phenomena*, not truth. To check "it is raining," we look outside â€” but we could be deceived by a fancy sprinkler. We never access truth directly; we access phenomena that "might imply something about truth (if any)."

3. **Justification's starting points**: Any justification must begin somewhere. The author identifies three possible starting points:
   - Phenomena (what we experience)
   - Already-justified beliefs
   - Truth (if directly accessible)

4. **Phenomena collapse into belief**: To use a phenomenon in justification, we must "digest" it â€” convert it into thought. The droplets falling mean nothing until interpreted. So phenomenon-based justification is actually belief-based.

5. **The regress problem**: If justification is always belief-based, then either:
   - Circular justification (unacceptable to most)
   - Infinite regress (impossible in finite life)
   - Grounding in unjustified belief
   
   The third option implies some "knowledge" is simply unjustified belief.

6. **Practical conclusion**: In practice, "knowledge" = "what each individual believes to be correct," extendable to "what most people in a field believe to be correct." This is sufficient for actual use.

### Kantian Observations

**Deep resonance with critical philosophy**: The author's central claim â€” that we never access truth directly but only phenomena â€” is structurally Kantian. We know appearances, not things-in-themselves. The author reaches this through practical analysis of epistemic activity rather than transcendental deduction.

**Key difference**: Kant would not conclude that knowledge is "just belief." The critical project saves objective validity for experience precisely by showing that phenomena are structured by a priori forms. The author doesn't engage with this â€” they seem to move from "we only access phenomena" to "therefore truth is methodologically inaccessible" without considering whether phenomena themselves have necessary structure.

**The digestion point**: The claim that phenomena must be "digested" into thought before use resonates with Kant's dictum: "Thoughts without content are empty, intuitions without concepts are blind." But for Kant, this isn't a limitation â€” it's the condition for experience being possible at all.

**Justification bases sidenote**: The author lists various justification bases (evidence, a priori justification, innate cognitive structures, etc.) and notes that "innate cognitive structures manifest only through shaping phenomena we experience." This is remarkably close to Kant's forms of intuition and categories â€” but the author treats this as phenomena-based (contingent), not as constitutive (necessary).

**Missing move**: The author asks "how do we access X?" for each justification base. Kant's question is different: "What must be the case for experience to be possible?" The transcendental turn isn't taken.

**Translation consideration**: The essay is already in English. Key terms: "phenomenon/phenomena" used in a quasi-technical sense (closer to Kant's "appearances" than to ordinary usage), "digestion" as a term for the conceptualization of experience.
