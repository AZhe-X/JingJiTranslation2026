### 2. A Thought on Knowledge

**Summary**: A direct engagement with epistemology. The author argues the JTB definition is methodologically problematic because we have no access to truthâ€”only phenomena.

**Key epistemological claims**:

1. **Belief is intrinsically subjective**, irrelevant to "objective truth" (if any). Connection to truth is *extrinsic*.

2. **The truth condition is methodologically impossible**: To verify "it's raining," we can only check memory, weather reports, or look outsideâ€”never check against *truth itself*. We might be deceived by fancy sprinklers.

3. **Phenomena require digestion**: To use a phenomenon in justification, we must convert it into thought. Therefore justification based on phenomena is really justification based on (digested) beliefs.

4. **The regress problem**: If all knowledge requires justification, and justification is belief about belief, then either:
   - Circular justification (problematic)
   - Grounded in unjustified beliefs (which we wouldn't use as starting points)
   - Therefore: some "knowledge" cannot be justified

5. **Pragmatic conclusion**: In practice, knowledge = "what each individual believes to be correct" or "what most people in a field believe to be correct."

**Sidenotesâ€”important**:
- **High energy physics example**: Fundamental particles are never directly observed; we get electromagnetic signals, reconstruct via models, the "best" model wins. The Standard Model is consensus belief with standards.
- **Mathematics example**: Mathematicians typically don't have direct access to mathematical truth; they prove from starting points taken to be true, using methods taken to be true.

**My assessment**: This is sophisticated. The author isn't naive skepticâ€”they're pointing out that the *practice* of knowledge-acquisition doesn't match the JTB *theory*. The "digestion" problem (phenomena â†’ belief conversion) anticipates issues in foundationalist epistemology. The pragmatic turn toward "what the field believes" resembles social epistemology.

However, the author may be conflating different things: (1) the analysis of knowledge, (2) how we verify claims, (3) the practice of inquiry. The Gettier problem shows JTB is insufficient, but doesn't show the truth condition is *methodologically impossible*â€”just that we can satisfy JTB accidentally without having knowledge.

### 1. Access as Fundamental
Throughout: we can only access phenomena. Truth, noumena, others' minds, the "objective world"â€”all beyond direct access. This isn't skepticism but methodology: work with what you have.

### 3. The Digestion Problem

## Questions and Tensions (First Reading)

1. **Can introspection really verify structural claims?** The author assumes introspection is reliable for structural positions. But introspection is notoriously unreliable for processes, beliefs, etc.

2. **Is category theory the right formalism?** The author acknowledges it's a choice. But different formalisms might reveal different structures.

3. **The a priori question**: If S_E is stable across evolution, is it a priori? The author is careful not to commit, but the framework invites the question.

4. **Social epistemology**: The "what most in the field believe" account of practical knowledge needs more development. How do fields form? How do they err?

5. **The normative question**: The framework is descriptive. But we want to know: which constructions *should* we make? The author explicitly doesn't answer this.

## Revised Overall Assessment

The framework is more robust than my first reading suggested. Key strengths I underappreciated:

2. **Acknowledgment of non-rationality**: The normative gap is deliberateâ€”the framework explicitly places normative grounding in non-rational drives, which is honest rather than evasive.

4. **Cross-cultural applicability**: Can the framework handle radically different conceptual schemes? The examples are largely Western or transcultural (math, physics).
