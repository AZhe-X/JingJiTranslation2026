## Essay 2: A Thought on Knowledge

**Core argument:**
A pragmatic/phenomenological critique of the classical definition of knowledge as "justified true belief" â€” examining what actually happens when humans form, check, and justify knowledge.

**Key philosophical moves:**

1. **Belief is intrinsically subjective**: As thought, belief is subjective. Its connection to "objective truth" (if any) is extrinsic â€” mediated through phenomena.

2. **We access phenomena, not truth**: To verify "it is raining outside," we can only look outside â€” but we see the *phenomenon* of rain (which could be a fancy sprinkler). We never compare belief directly against truth.

3. **The truth condition is methodologically impossible**: In practice, we cannot use knowledge against truth directly. We check memory, consult reports, observe phenomena â€” all beliefs or phenomena, never truth itself.

4. **Justification bases collapse**: Starting points for justification must be: (a) justified beliefs, (b) phenomena, or (c) truths. 5. **The regress problem**: If all justification is belief-based, then either (a) circular justification, or (b) grounded in unjustified beliefs, or (c) infinite regress (impossible in finite life). This suggests knowledge may simply be... belief.

6. **Practical conclusion**: In practice, what exists is "what each individual believes to be correct." Extendable to "what most people in a field believe to be correct" â€” a working definition of knowledge.
