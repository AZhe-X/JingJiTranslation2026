## Translation Principles

1. **Preserve phenomenological weight**: These are not ordinary words but technical philosophical terms with specific meanings in the Western tradition. Translations should signal this weight.

2. **Respect independent achievement**: The author arrived at these positions independently, not through direct engagement with Western phenomenology. Avoid over-assimilating to Husserl/Heidegger; note resonances without claiming derivation.

3. **Formal precision for category theory**: Mathematical terms (morphism, universal property, natural transformation) should use standard English mathematical terminology to ensure technical readers can follow the formalization.

4. **Consistent terminology**: Once a translation choice is made for a key term, use it consistently throughout. Variations confuse readers and obscure structural parallels.

5. **Context sensitivity**: Some terms (like å­˜åœ¨, ç»éªŒ) have both ordinary and philosophical uses. Let context determine the appropriate translation level.

6. **Preserve Chinese philosophical resonances where relevant**: Terms like å¢ƒ and è¯—æ„ carry resonances from classical Chinese philosophy (æ„å¢ƒ, å¢ƒç•Œ, dichterisch wohnen). Acknowledge these in notes without allowing them to dominate the translation.

7. **Avoid over-translation**: Do not add interpretive weight the original doesn't carry. If the author is deliberately ambiguous or open-ended, preserve that.

The author's style moves between formal precision (category theory), phenomenological description, and poetic-philosophical reflection. English translation should preserve this range:

- **Formal passages**: Use precise mathematical/philosophical terminology
- **Phenomenological descriptions**: Use clear, experience-near language that invites the reader to verify through introspection
- **Poetic passages**: Preserve metaphorical and affective language (æ¢¦å¢ƒ, è¯—æ„, æ²‰é†‰, æ¸…é†’)
