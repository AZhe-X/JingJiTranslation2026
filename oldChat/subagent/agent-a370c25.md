### 3. How to handle the Essay 3 punchline specifically

The Essay 3 punchline ("no one who owns a cat has ever felt absurd about it") depends on the reader's own recognition — it is a phenomenological demonstration that works by triggering the reader's first-person experience. Telling them the conclusion in advance transforms a first-person discovery into a third-person claim, which fundamentally changes the epistemic status of the experience.

My recommendation: In the Preface, describe Essay 3 ONLY as "three cases of repetitive labor, arranged in a specific order, producing a recognition about the nature of absurdity." Do not name the cat. Do not name the conclusion. The reader arrives knowing only that Essay 3 will do something experiential about absurdity.

In the Afterword, discuss the cat example freely, including the philosophy-of-mind analysis: the progression from externalized meaning (Sisyphus, the office worker) to internally generated meaning (the cat owner) demonstrates that absurdity is not a feature of repetitive action per se but of the structural relationship between one's Jing and the source of meaning. The other-minds dimension can also be discussed here: the cat example works partly because the cat's "meaning" is not something we project from external validation — it is felt directly, without the inference problem that plagues our attribution of humaneness to others (the same mechanism analyzed in Essay 4).
