## Claude at 2026-02-11 01:52:44

This is a **bad idea**. Here's why, briefly:

1. **The Chinese example is doing irreplaceable philosophical work.** The 道德仁义礼 sequence isn't an illustration of a point that could be made with any example — it *is* the point. It demonstrates how a tradition can have a structured conceptual vocabulary where each term defines the others relationally. No Western metaphysics example replicates that specific structure (a descending chain of emanation-like concepts where each term's meaning is constituted by its position in the sequence).

2. **Swapping it out contradicts the book's own methodology.** If the text argues for cross-traditional philosophical engagement, then retreating to Western-only examples when translation gets hard would be self-undermining. The difficulty *is* the lesson — it shows readers exactly why translation apparatus matters.

3. **The translation apparatus is not a bug, it's a feature.** TNs, constitutive character glosses, and contextual framing are what make the example *work* for English readers. A Western example that "readers already understand" wouldn't demonstrate the need for the translation methodology the text is presumably advocating.

4. **Relegating it to a TN or appendix signals that non-Western philosophy is supplementary.** That's a bad look for any serious cross-traditional work and undermines the author's credibility with readers in comparative philosophy.

**Better approach:** Keep the example. Tighten the translation apparatus so it feels integrated rather than burdensome. A well-placed two-sentence gloss before the passage, plus lean TNs, should be enough. The reader should feel challenged but not lost.
