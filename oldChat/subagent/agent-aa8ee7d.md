Good questions. Let me address each directly.

**1. Does "the poetic" adequately capture the phenomenological dimension? Risk of collapsing into Heidegger's Dichtung?**

"The poetic" does carry a Heideggerian gravitational pull, but I think this is manageable and may even be productive. The risk is real: an English-language reader trained in continental philosophy will immediately hear "dichterisch wohnen" (poetic dwelling) from Holderlin/Heidegger. But here is why I think it works despite this:

The author's 诗意 and Heidegger's Dichtung share a genuine family resemblance -- both describe a mode of engagement with the world that is non-instrumental, meaning-constitutive, and aware of its own finitude. The reader who makes the Heidegger connection is not *wrong*, they are seeing a real convergence. The divergence (which the translator's introduction or footnotes can clarify) is that: (a) the author arrives at 诗意 through post-nihilistic reconstruction, not through meditation on language and poetry; (b) the author's 诗意 is more agentic -- we actively *construct* the 境 in which meaning arises, rather than *dwelling* in a clearing that is given; (c) the author's 诗意 is explicitly ludic -- the game, the dreamscape, the coin-toss -- whereas Heidegger's Dichtung has a more solemn register.

These differences are significant but they are differences *within* the same conceptual neighborhood. "The poetic" places the reader in the right neighborhood and the text itself will do the work of specifying the author's particular address within it.

**2. Does "the poetic" convey the non-spectatorial, fully-engaged-yet-aware quality?**

This is the strongest concern. Husserl's transcendental attitude is spectatorial -- you step back to observe the structures of consciousness. The author's 诗意 is the opposite: you step *in* while remaining aware. The author's key formulation is "清醒的梦境" -- a lucid dreamscape. You are fully immersed, yet you know it is a dreamscape.

"The poetic" does convey this better than most alternatives, because poetry itself involves this double consciousness: the poet is fully immersed in the image/rhythm/feeling while simultaneously crafting the structure that produces it. The reader of poetry likewise inhabits the poem's world while knowing it is a poem. This is exactly the structure the author describes.

Where "the poetic" falls slightly short is in conveying the *active construction* dimension. Poetry in English connotes receptivity and sensitivity more than construction and agency. The author's 诗意 includes the moment of building your office desk arrangement, choosing your clothes, pressing the shutter at sunset -- acts of *making* a 境. "The poetic" captures the awareness dimension better than the making dimension.

However, I cannot think of a single English term that captures both. And the author's own text will demonstrate the constructive dimension through examples. So the term does not need to carry the full weight alone.

**3. Any alternative from the phenomenological tradition?**

I considered three phenomenological alternatives and rejected all of them in favor of "the poetic":

- **"Lucid engagement"** or **"lucid inhabitation"**: Captures the 清醒 (lucid) dimension perfectly but loses the 诗 (poetic) dimension entirely. The author deliberately chose a word with aesthetic resonance. Stripping that away would be a loss.

- **"Transcendental praxis"**: Captures the post-Husserlian, practice-oriented character but sounds like academic jargon. It would kill the literary register of the 诗意 essay, which is itself one of the most poetically written pieces in the collection.

- **"Poietic awareness"**: From the Greek poiesis (making), this would capture both the creative/constructive dimension and the awareness dimension. But "poietic" is obscure, easily confused with "poetic," and carries Aristotelian baggage (poiesis as instrumental making vs. praxis as self-constituting action). The author's 诗意 is closer to praxis than to poiesis in the Aristotelian sense, so this would actually mislead.

**Verdict: I endorse "the poetic."** It is the best available option. It places the reader in the right conceptual neighborhood, it has the right register (neither too technical nor too casual), it is grammatically flexible, and its limitations (slight Heideggerian gravitational pull, slight underemphasis on active construction) are correctable through context and annotation.

One suggestion: on first occurrence, consider a brief parenthetical or footnote along the lines of: "诗意, here rendered as 'the poetic,' denotes a mode of lucid engagement with constituted meaning-structures -- fully invested yet aware of their constructed character. It shares ground with Heidegger's 'poetic dwelling' but emphasizes active construction over receptive inhabitation." This would inoculate against the Heideggerian over-reading without burdening the main text.
