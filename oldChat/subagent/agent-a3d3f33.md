This is an interesting question from the author. Let me think about this carefully based on my reading notes.

Yes, I did notice this progression, and I documented it explicitly in my Memo.md during Step 1. Under "Part 1 Cross-Cutting Observations," point 5, I wrote:

> **Progression**: Essay 1 (ontology of collective vs. individual) -> Essay 2 (epistemology: what can the individual know?) -> Essay 3 (existential: what about meaning?). The progression from ontology to epistemology to existential meaning is deliberate.

I agree with the characterization, and I'd add a few observations from the translation perspective:

1. **The progression is also a progression of the individual's relationship to itself.** Essay 1: the individual as starting point for understanding others. Essay 2: the individual as the only epistemic agent (knowledge = what the individual believes). Essay 3: the individual as the source of meaning (internally generated, not sought from outside). Each essay deepens the commitment to the individual as phenomenological center.

2. **The progression mirrors the structure of Part 2 in miniature.** Part 2 goes: metaphysical framework (Essay 4, which covers ontology) -> meaning/purpose (Essay 5, existential) -> formal structure of experience (Essays 6-7, which formalize the epistemological and ontological claims). The fish/fishing metaphor holds: Part 1 gives three concrete instances of the progression; Part 2 gives the methodology that generates it.

3. **For translation, this means the Part 1 essay ordering is load-bearing** — not just thematically but architecturally. The reader needs to move through "what exists" -> "what can I know" -> "what does it mean" in that order, because each essay's conclusion becomes the premise of the next. Essay 1's conclusion (the individual can only understand from its own standpoint) is the premise of Essay 2 (knowledge is what the individual believes). Essay 2's conclusion (knowledge reduces to belief/phenomena) is the premise of Essay 3 (meaning cannot come from outside, only from within).

I should note that several experts also picked up on related aspects of this structure, though none used exactly the "ontology -> epistemology -> existential" framing. The Kantian expert noted the parallel to Kant's three Critiques (pure reason, practical reason, judgment). The existentialism expert traced how the "individual as starting point" theme builds across all three.
