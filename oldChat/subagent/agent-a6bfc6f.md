**AMEND** — with two specific changes, otherwise agree.

**Amendment 1: Edit 4 (Essay 4 reversal) — I disagree with keeping it fully as-is.**

The current text reveals the *specific content* of both directions: "phenomenal ritual conduct generates the concept of humaneness, which generates the concept of character, which generates the concept of the Way." This spells out the bottom-up direction step by step. The reader who encounters this in the Preface already knows *exactly* what the reversal contains before they read Essay 4 — they just need to follow along and confirm what they've already been told.

The experience the essay produces is not merely knowing *that* a reversal occurs — it's the experience of *tracking each step yourself* and feeling the accumulated weight of the construction as it builds. Knowing in advance that "phenomenal ritual conduct generates humaneness generates character generates the Way" flattens this into verification rather than discovery.

**Proposed change:** Keep the first and last sentences. Remove the parenthetical content that specifies both directions:

"In Essay 4, the author walks through the Dao-De-Ren-Yi-Li hierarchy twice, in opposite directions. The reader's experience of seeing the entire logical order reverse itself is the essay's central demonstration. The reversal must be experienced sequentially to produce its effect."

This tells the reader: there is a hierarchy, it gets traversed twice, the directions are opposite, and the experience matters. It does *not* tell them what the "actual" direction is. The reader knows a reversal is coming but must discover its content themselves.

I recognize that the essay itself announces the reversal before performing it — but the essay announces it *after* the reader has already encountered the top-down reading and felt its plausibility. The Preface delivers both directions simultaneously before either has been experienced.

**Amendment 2: Afterword Section B — refine the metaphysics representation.**

The Kantian parallels section should include one additional point: the author's treatment of Zeroth Reality deserves explicit comparison to Kant's noumenon, but with a crucial difference. For Kant, the thing-in-itself is epistemically inaccessible but ontologically necessary. For this author, the Zeroth Reality is *structurally self-undermining*: the moment it is articulated, it becomes Second Reality. This is more radical than Kant — it's not that we *cannot* reach the noumenon, but that any reaching *constitutes* it as construction. This is arguably the collection's single most important metaphysical claim and should be prominently featured in the Afterword.

Everything else — the Preface Edits 1-3, the Afterword structure and scope, the placement, the tone, the length — I fully agree with. The editor's synthesis is excellent work.
