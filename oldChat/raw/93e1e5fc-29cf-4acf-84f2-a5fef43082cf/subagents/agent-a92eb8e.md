### Guiding Principle

The Preface should function like a trail map: it shows the terrain and marks the path, but it doesn't describe the view from the summit. The Afterword is where you sit down after the hike and discuss what you saw.

### 1. What to remove/soften in the Preface

**A. Line 46 — the three-item list in Fish and Fishing:**

CURRENT: "Part 1 provides the reader with concrete philosophical *experiences* -- of recognizing emergent phenomena, of watching justified true belief collapse, of feeling the absurd dissolve in the presence of a cat."

PROPOSED: "Part 1 provides the reader with concrete philosophical *experiences* -- of recognizing emergent phenomena, of confronting the limits of justified belief, and of testing a familiar existential claim against ordinary life."

Rationale: The current version delivers three conclusions (emergent phenomena are *recognized*, JTB *collapses*, absurdity *dissolves*). The revision names three *activities* (recognizing, confronting, testing) without revealing outcomes. "Testing a familiar existential claim against ordinary life" is deliberately vague — the reader doesn't know which claim, or what the test yields. The cat is removed entirely here; it appears later in the Experiential Sequences section anyway.

**B. Line 50 — structural claims paragraph:**

CURRENT: The full paragraph stating nihilism-as-consequence and absurdity-as-structural-collapse with mechanisms.

PROPOSED: "Two structural claims of the collection deserve advance notice. First, the author offers a distinctive account of the relationship between nihilism and metaphysics — one that treats the former as a consequence of the latter rather than its negation. Second, the author proposes an alternative to the existentialist treatment of absurdity, developed through the experiential progression of Essay 3. Both claims are best encountered through the essays themselves; the Translator's Afterword discusses them in full."

Rationale: This names the *topics* (nihilism/metaphysics relationship; an alternative to existentialist absurdity) and even hints at the *direction* (consequence not negation) without revealing the mechanism (Jing seeking meaning externally), the resolution (inhabiting a Jing with internally generated meaning), or the specific demonstration (cat ownership). The forward reference to the Afterword signals that the deeper discussion exists but is placed where it won't spoil. I kept "consequence rather than negation" as a minimal hint because without *some* content this paragraph would be too empty to justify its existence — and this particular claim is more of a framing orientation than a punchline.

**C. Lines 95-96 — Essay 3 example in Experiential Sequences:**

### 2. What the Afterword should contain

**Scope:** The Translator's Afterword is the post-experience companion to the Preface. Where the Preface orients the reader before the journey, the Afterword reflects on it after. It should contain three kinds of material:

**A. Moved spoiler content (expanded):**
- The full nihilism-as-consequence-of-metaphysics analysis, now with freedom to discuss the mechanism and connect it to Essay 4's demonstration
- The full absurdity-as-structural-collapse analysis, with the cat example discussed explicitly, and the comparison to Camus's revolt developed
- The Essay 3 punchline, now analyzable because the reader has experienced it
- The property-to-definition reversal (from the Dao-De passage) as the collection's most original philosophical move

**B. Cross-traditional connections:**
- Resonances with Heidegger (poetic dwelling vs. the Poetic — emphasizing the author's greater agentiveness)
- Resonances with Kant (stable structures vs. a priori categories; the "another kind of Kantian question" the author raises in the Appendix)
- Resonances with Madhyamaka (conventional truth / ultimate truth vs. Second Reality / Zeroth Reality; the Diamond Sutra dream-simile vs. dreamscape)
- Resonances with Wittgenstein (language games, forms of life, and the author's formalization of understanding as natural transformation)
- Resonances with Husserl (phenomena as starting point, but without the epoché)
- Where the author *departs* from each tradition — this is as important as the resonances

**C. Translation choices and their stakes:**
- The decision to leave Jing untranslated and what it costs/gains
- The 德 virtue/character split and why it matters philosophically, not just terminologically
- The meme replacement in Essay 7 — the full rationale for experiential fidelity over literal accuracy
- The "phenomenal X" convention and its philosophical implications
- Any other consequential choices that emerged during translation

**Structure:** I'd propose 4-5 sections, roughly:
1. A brief opening (1 paragraph) — now that you've read the essays, here is what we can discuss freely
2. The collection's central arguments (moved spoiler content, expanded)
3. The author in conversation (cross-traditional connections)
4. On translating these essays (consequential choices)
5. A brief closing

**Tone:** Same register as the Preface — scholarly but not academic, the translator's voice, not a critic's. The Afterword should feel like a conversation with a knowledgeable guide after you've both walked the same trail.

**Length:** I'd estimate 2,000-3,000 words — roughly the same as the Preface. It shouldn't be a dissertation; it should be a thoughtful reflection.

### 3. How to handle the Essay 3 punchline specifically

The cat punchline is the single most damaging spoiler because it is an *experiential conclusion* that depends on the reader's own recognition. Unlike the nihilism/metaphysics claim (which is propositional and can be pre-stated without full damage), the cat recognition is phenomenological — it works by producing an "of course!" moment that cannot be replicated if the reader arrives already knowing the answer.

My approach: in the Preface, the cat owner is named as the third case (Sisyphus, office worker, cat owner) — this is fine, it's structural information. But the *recognition* ("no one who owns a cat has ever felt absurd about it") is removed entirely. In the Afterword, this becomes a centerpiece: we can discuss *why* the recognition works, *what* it demonstrates about the structure of absurdity, and *how* it connects to the formal apparatus of Part 2.

### 4. Content that should stay despite being somewhat revelatory

- **Line 44** (Part 2 concept list): "developing the concepts of Jing, the metaphysical, constancy, the poetic, and the categorical formalism that structures them" — this names Part 2's topics. The reader needs to know what's coming. Naming concepts is not spoiling arguments.
- **Line 46 revised** (three activities): The reader should know that Part 1 involves ontology, epistemology, and existential meaning. These are topic markers, not conclusions.
- **Line 50 revised** (nihilism/metaphysics as topics): A minimal hint of the direction. Without it, the paragraph is too empty.
- **Lines 97-99** (Essay 4 and Essay 7 examples): As argued above.
- **The entire Experiential Sequences section (lines 89-101):** This section's *purpose* — explaining that the collection works through experience, not just argument — must remain. It is the Preface's most important contribution. The fix is surgical: remove the punchlines, keep the method-description.

### 5. New content the Afterword should add beyond moved spoilers

- **The "two questions" from the Appendix:** The author explicitly names "How is philosophy possible?" and "How is meaning possible?" as the collection's guiding questions. These are discussed only in the Appendix (Essay 8), too late for many readers to appreciate their structuring role. The Afterword can make this explicit.
- **The collection as a philosophical experiment on the reader:** The Preface says the collection "enacts the very progression it describes." The Afterword can analyze *how* — how the Fish/Fishing structure is itself a Jing constructed for the reader, and how reading the collection sequentially is itself an instance of the post-metaphysical practice the collection advocates.
- **Open questions:** What the collection does *not* resolve — intersubjectivity beyond structural alignment, the status of ethics, the relationship between multiple Jing-structures in a community. These point outward from the collection and invite further work.
