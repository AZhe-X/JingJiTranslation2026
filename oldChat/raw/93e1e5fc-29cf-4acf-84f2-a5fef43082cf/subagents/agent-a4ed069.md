## Claude at 2026-02-11 03:56:39

Analysis sent to the team lead. Here's a summary of my findings:

**Key discovery:** The author defines 感受 explicitly in two places:
- Essay 5 (诗意), fn2: 感受 has two levels — (1) raw sensory input/stimulus, and (2) the "crystallization, surge, turbulence" that stimuli constitute. Both are 感受.
- Essay 6 (范畴论与境), line 166: 感受 and 体验 are explicitly equated as "any part of P [phenomena]." The totality of 感受 IS phenomena.

**Recommendation:** Context-dependent translation:
- **"Feeling"** in first-person affective/phenomenal descriptions (the majority of uses, including the 道德仁义礼 passage)
- **"Experience"** in technical/categorical contexts (Essays 6-7, where 感受 = any element of the phenomenal field)
- **经验** is a distinct term — accumulated empirical data (Erfahrung), never interchangeable with 感受

The author deliberately uses a single Chinese term to span sensation through integrated experience, refusing to split what phenomenological traditions separate. "Feeling" in English replicates this breadth without importing theoretical commitments the author doesn't invoke.
