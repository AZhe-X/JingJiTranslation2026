### 3. How to handle the Essay 3 punchline specifically

The cat example's philosophical power lies in its *ordinariness*. The reader must arrive at the recognition themselves: "Wait — no one feels absurd about owning a cat. Why not? It's structurally identical to Sisyphus. So what's different?" This recognition is the philosophical payload, and it works precisely because the reader generates it from their own experience rather than receiving it as a thesis.

In the **Preface**: Hint that Essay 3 uses everyday examples to address a philosophical problem. Do not name the cat. Do not state the conclusion. The Experiential Sequences section can say that the essay uses a "progression from mythic to mundane" and that "the sequence cannot be rearranged" — this tells the reader to pay attention without telling them what they will find.

In the **Afterword**: Discuss the cat example freely and in full. Analyze why it works. Draw the connection to Husserl's eidetic variation (varying conditions to isolate essential structures). Note that it functions as what the author would later call a "philosophical experiment" in the K1 sense. This is the right place for this discussion because the reader has already undergone the experience.
