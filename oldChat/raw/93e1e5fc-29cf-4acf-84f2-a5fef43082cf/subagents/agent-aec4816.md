## Review of Essay4-DaoDeRenYiLi Draft

This is strong work. The argument's logical architecture survives the translation clearly. Below are my comments, organized from most to least important. I focus on language-game structure, the grammar of "explanation," and places where the English prose could more precisely track the Chinese logical moves.

---

### 1. The word "explanation" (解释) — the pivot the whole argument turns on

The argument's engine is the double role of 解释: (a) I *explain* phenomenal ritual conduct by positing inner feeling (phenomenal humaneness) and motivation (phenomenal rightness), and then (b) this explanatory relationship gets *reversed* and presented as a generative one. The word 解释 appears five times in the Chinese passage (lines 24, 26) and each occurrence marks a specific move in the argument.

Your draft handles this well, but I want to flag one place where the English could be tighter. In line 42:

> "I have merely **applied** my explanation of '**my own** phenomenal ritual conduct' to the phenomenal ritual conduct we observe **others** performing."

The Chinese (line 26) says: 仅仅是将我对"**自己**的'现象礼'"的解释**套用**到了我们看到的**别人**所行的"现象礼"上.

"Applied" is fine for 套用, but 套用 is more pointed than "apply" — it means something like "template-applied," "imposed by analogy," "cookie-cuttered onto." The critical move is that I take a first-person explanation (my feeling → my motivation → my action) and *impose it as a template* onto third-person observations where I have no access to the inner states. "Applied" is slightly too neutral for this. Consider: "**projected** my explanation of my own phenomenal ritual conduct onto..." or "**imposed** my explanation... onto..." This would better signal the epistemically illegitimate leap the author is identifying.

---

### 2. The "can → should" transformation — the argument's climax

This is the single most important logical move in the passage. The Chinese (line 26) has:

> 事实上是"人都是（**可以**）有仁的" ... 但是我们最终对其的理解是"人都是（**应该**）有仁的"——或者说，"有仁的才是人"

Your draft (line 44):

> "all people (**can**) possess humaneness" ... "all people (**should**) possess humaneness" — or rather, "only those who possess humaneness count as human."

This is accurate. But consider whether the parenthetical emphasis markers (**can**, **should**) are doing enough work in English. In the Chinese, the bold is doing contrastive stress: 可以 vs. 应该. In English, the parentheses slightly *diminish* the force — they make the words look like qualifications rather than the crux. One option: remove parentheses and use italics for contrastive stress:

> "all people *can* possess humaneness" ... "all people *should* possess humaneness"

This makes the contrast hit harder. The parentheses in the Chinese work because the surrounding structure (人都是...有仁的) stays constant and the reader's eye catches the substitution. In English, the same effect is achieved better with italics.

---

### 3. "That which makes a person a person" — the grammar of 德

Line 40:

> "we take what we believe (ought to be) universally possessed — here, phenomenal humaneness — and call it 'character.'"

This is the moment 德 enters as a technical concept. But the Chinese (line 24) does more — it says:

> 接下来，我们便把我们认为（应当是）人人皆而有之的东西，在这里便是"现象仁"，称之为"德"

The phrase 人人皆而有之的东西 is crucial: "that which everyone-all-and-has." It is not just "universally possessed" (which sounds like a statistical claim) but "that which should be universal" — already normative. Your "what we believe (ought to be) universally possessed" captures the normative dimension with "(ought to be)" but again the parentheses mute it. Consider:

> "we take what we believe ought to be universally possessed — here, phenomenal humaneness — and call it 'character.'"

Removing the parentheses lets "ought to be" carry its full normative weight in the sentence.

---

### 4. "Buried from view" — excellent

Line 46: "the logic by which phenomenal ritual conduct generated 'the Way' has been **buried from view**" for 掩埋不见. This is very good. It captures both the concealment (掩埋) and the invisibility (不见). Well done.

### 5. The final sentence — "pursuing the branches and forgetting the root"

Line 48: "pursuing the branches and forgetting the root" for 逐末而忘本. This works well as a translation. If you want to signal to the reader that this is a classical Chinese idiom (not the author's coinage), a brief parenthetical or TN might help — but it is not essential. The English reads naturally.

### 6. Footnote 3 — very well handled

The footnote (line 54) faithfully renders the author's self-conscious admission that 仁义礼 is already a metaphysical framework being used to describe phenomena — a kind of hermeneutic circle. The explanation of 义 as the bridge between feeling and action is clear. No changes needed.

---

### 7. Footnote 24 — the "canvas" metaphor

Line 58: "something like the canvas on which a painting is made" for 像是对于一幅画而言的画纸一样. "Canvas" is fine, though the Chinese says 画纸 (painting paper), which is humbler — 画纸 is just paper, not stretched canvas. The distinction is minor but: "something like the paper on which a painting is made" would be slightly more faithful. The point is that 道 is the *condition of possibility* (the surface), not itself part of the content (the painting). "Paper" conveys this unassuming substrate quality better than "canvas," which in English can connote artistic seriousness.

Line 64: "Translation adapted from Ivanhoe (2003)" — this is exactly right per our convention. The comparison with Ivanhoe's rendering and the justification for our departures (humaneness rather than benevolence, etc.) is well done.

### 9. TN3 — the 德 shift note

Line 68: Good. The note explains the shift from "virtue" to "(constitutive) character" clearly. One small addition to consider: you might add that 德 applies to *all things*, not just persons — "all things have their character (凡事诸物皆有其德)" — since this universality is what decisively separates the author's usage from both Confucian moral virtue and Aristotelian arete.

---

### 10. One missing nuance: 失 (shi, "loss")

The Laozi quote hinges on 失 ("to lose"). The author later plays on this same word at line 28: 已经全部**失**去了 — the logic "has been entirely **lost**." This echo is philosophically significant: the 失 in the Laozi (loss of Dao → virtue → humaneness → etc.) is reinterpreted as the loss of the *constructive logic itself*. Your draft translates both correctly — "lost" in the quote, "lost" in line 46 — but the reader may not notice the echo because the English sentences are far apart. Consider whether a brief TN could flag this: "The author's use of 'lost' (失) here deliberately echoes the Laozi quote: the original 失 describes the loss of the Way; the author's 失 describes the loss of the very logic by which the Way was constructed." This is optional but would reward an attentive reader.

---

### Summary

The draft is solid. The argument's logical structure is preserved. My main recommendations:

1. **"Projected" or "imposed" for 套用** (instead of "applied") — captures the epistemic overreach
2. **Remove parentheses around "can" / "should" / "ought to be"** — let the contrastive stress land in English via italics
3. **"Paper" instead of "canvas"** for 画纸 in footnote 24 — minor fidelity point
4. **Optional TN on the 失 echo** — connects the Laozi quote to the author's conclusion
5. **Add universality to TN3** — mention 凡事诸物皆有其德 to complete the picture of 德

Everything else reads well. The TNs are well-placed and proportionate.
