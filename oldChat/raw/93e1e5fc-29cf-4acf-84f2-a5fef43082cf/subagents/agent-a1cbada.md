## Claude at 2026-02-10 23:57:41

A translator's note is warranted but should be brief and placed only at the first occurrence. Here is my recommendation:

**Yes, include a TN -- but make it light.**

The risk of confusion is real and specific: a philosophically literate English reader encountering "category theory" in a text already saturated with Kantian vocabulary will reasonably wonder whether the author means Kant's *Kategorien* (the twelve pure concepts of the understanding) or the mathematical discipline (Eilenberg-Mac Lane, objects and morphisms, functors). The author's deliberate exploitation of this overlap makes the disambiguation more necessary, not less -- precisely because the resonance is intentional, the reader needs to know which sense is primary and that the echo is purposeful.

**Suggested TN (at first occurrence of 范畴论):**

> **TN:** "Category theory" (范畴论) here refers to the mathematical discipline founded by Eilenberg and Mac Lane, not to Kant's table of categories (*Kategorien*), though the author's use of the term is alive to both senses.

This accomplishes three things in one sentence:
1. Disambiguates the primary referent (mathematical category theory)
2. Acknowledges the Kantian resonance without over-explaining it
3. Signals to the reader that the dual meaning is authorial, not accidental

**Why it is not patronizing:** The confusion is domain-specific, not a matter of general intelligence. Category theory is standard in mathematics but unusual in Continental philosophy. A reader fluent in Kant but unfamiliar with mathematics (or vice versa) will genuinely benefit. The note respects the reader by being concise and substantive rather than didactic.

**Where to place it:** Footnote at first occurrence only. Do not repeat. If the dictionary already has an entry for 范畴 / 范畴论, a cross-reference ("see Glossary") can replace a longer note.
